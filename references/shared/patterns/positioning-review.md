# Pattern: Positioning Review

## Applicable Situation

Use when a GTM plan, launch, sales narrative, or messaging strategy depends on clear B2B positioning.

## Inputs

- Product or company being positioned
- Competitive alternatives
- Buyer champion
- Differentiated capabilities
- Business value
- Intended sales motion

## Default Workflow

1. Decide what is being positioned: product, company, bundle, platform, or segment-specific offer.
2. Identify the real competitive alternatives, including status quo and no-decision.
3. Name the buyer champion and the value they personally need to defend.
4. Translate differentiated capability into buyer-understandable value by asking "so what?" until the value is concrete.
5. Remove objections from value themes; handle them in sales proof or risk reversal instead.
6. Align positioning with how the product is actually sold.

## Branch Rules

- For single-product companies, avoid separate company and product positioning unless customers experience them differently.
- For multi-product companies, let GTM motion decide whether to position a lead product, family, suite, or platform.
- If functions disagree on competitors, separate marketing-visible competitors, sales-shortlist competitors, future product competitors, and status quo.

## Anti-Patterns

- Positioning against the loudest competitor instead of the real buyer alternative.
- Claiming generic value such as "improve efficiency."
- Mixing value themes with objections like deployment risk or total cost of ownership.

## Acceptance Criteria

- The positioning states who it is for, what alternative it replaces, and why the buyer should care.
- Differentiated value is concrete enough for a champion to repeat.
- The positioning matches the intended GTM motion.

## Conflict Notes

Product teams often include horizon competitors; GTM positioning should prioritize current buyer alternatives and sales reality.
